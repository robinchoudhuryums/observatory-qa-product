## Dental Insurance Terminology

### Common Terms Staff Should Know
- **Annual Maximum** — The maximum dollar amount the insurance plan will pay for dental services in a plan year (typically $1,000-$2,500)
- **Deductible** — The amount the patient must pay out-of-pocket before insurance begins covering services (typically $25-$100/year)
- **Coinsurance** — The percentage split between insurance and patient (e.g., "80/20" means insurance pays 80%, patient pays 20%)
- **Waiting Period** — Time a patient must be enrolled before certain benefits are available (common for major services: 6-12 months)
- **Frequency Limitations** — How often insurance covers specific procedures:
  - Prophylaxis: typically 2x per year (some plans allow 3x for perio patients)
  - Bitewing X-rays: typically 1x per year
  - Full mouth X-rays/Panoramic: typically 1x per 3-5 years
  - Fluoride: typically 1-2x per year (often age-limited to under 16 or 19)
- **UCR (Usual, Customary, and Reasonable)** — The fee schedule the insurance company uses to determine coverage amounts
- **Pre-Authorization/Pre-Determination** — Submitting treatment plan to insurance for benefit verification before treatment (recommended for procedures over $300)
- **Coordination of Benefits (COB)** — When a patient has dual insurance coverage, determining which plan pays primary vs. secondary
- **Missing Tooth Clause** — Many plans do not cover replacement of teeth that were missing before the policy effective date
- **Downgrades** — Insurance may cover a less expensive alternative (e.g., amalgam fee for a composite filling)

### Typical Coverage Tiers
| Category | Coverage | Examples |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Preventive/Diagnostic (Class I) | 80-100% | Cleanings, exams, X-rays, fluoride |
| Basic (Class II) | 60-80% | Fillings, simple extractions, SRP |
| Major (Class III) | 50% | Crowns, bridges, dentures, implants |
| Orthodontics (Class IV) | 50% (lifetime max) | Braces, aligners (often child-only) |

## Emergency Triage Protocol

### Questions Every Emergency Call Must Include
1. **What happened?** — Onset and mechanism (sudden vs. gradual, trauma vs. spontaneous)
2. **Where is the pain?** — Specific tooth/area, radiating?
3. **How severe is the pain?** — Scale of 1-10
4. **When did it start?** — Hours, days, weeks?
5. **What makes it better or worse?** — Hot, cold, biting, lying down?
6. **Any swelling?** — Where, how much, spreading?
7. **Any bleeding?** — How much, continuous?
8. **Fever?** — Any elevated temperature?
9. **Allergies?** — Especially to antibiotics (penicillin, amoxicillin)
10. **Current medications?** — Blood thinners are critical to know

### Urgency Classification
| Level | Criteria | Response |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **IMMEDIATE / ER** | Uncontrolled bleeding, facial swelling compromising airway or vision, jaw fracture, avulsed permanent tooth (within 1 hour) | Direct to ER immediately |
| **Same-Day** | Severe pain (8-10), facial swelling with fever, avulsed tooth (>1 hour), broken tooth with exposed nerve, post-op bleeding not controlled by gauze | Schedule same-day emergency |
| **Within 24 Hours** | Moderate pain (5-7), lost crown/filling, broken tooth without nerve exposure, mild swelling without fever | Schedule next available or next day |
| **Next Available** | Mild pain (1-4), sensitivity, minor chip, loose restoration, orthodontic wire poking | Schedule within the week |

### Home Care Instructions (Front Desk Can Provide)
- **Pain**: Ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6 hours (if no allergies/contraindications) OR acetaminophen 500-1000mg every 6 hours. Alternate if needed. *Do not exceed 3000mg acetaminophen/day.*
- **Swelling**: Ice pack 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off
- **Avulsed tooth**: Place tooth in milk or saline, do NOT scrub the root, get to office or ER within 1 hour
- **Lost filling/crown**: Temporary dental cement from pharmacy (Dentemp), avoid chewing on that side
- **Bleeding after extraction**: Bite on damp gauze or tea bag for 30 minutes with firm pressure

**IMPORTANT**: Front desk should NEVER diagnose, recommend specific prescription medications, or advise patients to ignore symptoms. When in doubt, consult the dentist on call.

## HIPAA Compliance for Dental Offices

### Patient Identity Verification (Every Call)
Before discussing ANY patient information:
1. Verify **full name** (first and last)
2. Verify **date of birth**
3. If calling about another patient (parent/spouse), verify authorized representative status

### What Staff CAN Share on the Phone
- Appointment date and time (after identity verification)
- General office information (hours, location, accepted insurance)
- Treatment plan previously discussed (after identity verification)
- Account balance (after identity verification)

### What Staff CANNOT Share
- Patient information with unauthorized callers
- Clinical details with insurance companies without patient authorization
- Information about one patient with another patient (even family members without authorization)
- PHI via unsecured email or text

### Common HIPAA Violations in Dental Offices
- Calling out a patient's full name in the waiting room (use first name only or pager)
- Leaving appointment reminder voicemails with procedure details
- Discussing patient treatment in areas where other patients can overhear
- Sharing patient records with spouse/parent without signed authorization

### Tooth Surfaces
- **Mesial (M)** — Surface facing toward the midline
- **Distal (D)** — Surface facing away from the midline
- **Buccal (B)** — Surface facing the cheek
- **Lingual (L)** — Surface facing the tongue
- **Occlusal (O)** — Biting surface (posterior teeth)
- **Incisal (I)** — Biting edge (anterior teeth)
- **Facial (F)** — Front-facing surface (general term for buccal/labial)
